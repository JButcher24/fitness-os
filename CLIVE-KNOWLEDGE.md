# CLIVE — Coaching knowledge base

This is the evidence base Clive coaches from. It's the *principles*; `fitness-data.json`
is the *specifics*. Apply this to Jamie's real numbers — never recite it at him.

Everything here is scoped to **who Jamie actually is**, not generic bodybuilding.

## Who this is for (read before giving advice)

- A **beginner** lifter, ~8 months of untrained-ish potential still on the table.
- Currently **~263 lb, ~35% body fat, BMI ~36** (obese range) heading to **210 lb (15 st)**.
- Goal is **fat loss + body recomposition** — lose fat while building/keeping muscle.
- Trains **full-body**, sometimes **fasted**, and is **joint-aware** (the "tibia protocol":
  cleared to train, but lower-body load progresses on technique, not bravado).
- Food is tracked in **MyFitnessPal**, not here. Coach nutrition off the **protein floor**
  and the **scale trend**, not by micromanaging meals.

Because he's a novice carrying extra fat, **he is close to a best-case recomposition
candidate** — beginners in a calorie deficit routinely lose fat *and* gain muscle at the
same time, which stops being possible for lean, advanced lifters. Don't import advice built
for people who aren't him.

## What actually moves the needle (priority order)

Coach the top of this list first. Most people obsess over the bottom.

1. **Adherence** — the plan he'll actually stick to beats the "optimal" plan he won't.
2. **Energy balance** — a modest, sustained calorie deficit is what loses fat. Nothing else substitutes.
3. **Protein** — high enough to hold muscle while the fat comes off.
4. **Progressive overload** — the training signal that says "keep the muscle / build more".
5. **Sleep & recovery** — where the adaptation actually happens.
6. **Everything else** (supplements, meal timing, exercise selection minutiae) — rounding error by comparison.

## Fat loss

- **Deficit is the mechanism.** ~3,500 kcal ≈ 1 lb of fat as a rough rule (imperfect, but fine
  for coaching). A ~500 kcal/day deficit ≈ ~1 lb/week.
- **Rate:** aim **0.5–1% of bodyweight per week** — for Jamie that's the **1–2 lb/week pocket**
  already in the rules. Early weeks often drop faster; that's mostly water and glycogen, not fat.
  As he leans out, drift to the **slower** end to protect muscle.
- **Judge by the 7-day average, never the daily number.** Day-to-day weight swings 2–4 lb on
  water, sodium, carbs, bowels, sleep. The trend line is the only honest signal.
- **Plateaus are normal.** True stalls (no trend movement for ~2–3 weeks) mean the deficit has
  eroded — from smaller portions creeping up, less NEAT, or metabolic adaptation. Fix with the
  smallest lever first: tighten intake ~150–200 kcal *or* add steps. One change at a time, then wait.
- **Diet breaks help long cuts.** After several hard weeks, a planned week at maintenance can
  restore adherence, training performance, and hormones — without erasing progress. Not a
  cheat, a tool.
- **Metabolic adaptation is real but overblown.** The body gets a bit more efficient as you
  lose; it doesn't "break". Steps and protein blunt it.

## Protein & nutrition

- **Protein target ~1.6–2.2 g per kg of bodyweight**, higher end in a deficit to spare muscle.
  For Jamie, anchoring to **goal/lean weight** (not his current higher total) is smarter:
  his **219 g target / 180 g floor** lands right in the evidence-based zone. **The 180 g floor is
  the non-negotiable** — hit that even on bad days.
- **Total protein across the day matters far more than timing.** The post-fasted-session
  40–50 g rule is sound and worth keeping, but don't sweat exact "anabolic windows" beyond that.
- **Protein is also the most satiating macro** — it makes the deficit easier to hold. Another
  reason the floor comes first.
- **Prioritise whole foods and fibre** (25–35 g/day): fuller for fewer calories, better digestion,
  steadier energy. Carbs are not the enemy — they fuel the lifts; Jamie's plan carries them on purpose.
- **Alcohol** is empty calories that also blunt recovery and sleep. Not banned, but it's the first
  thing to trim when the trend stalls.
- **Fasted training is fine** for fat loss — no meaningful fat-burning advantage or disadvantage
  over fed training; it comes down to preference and performance. The one real cost is muscle
  protein breakdown pre-session, which is exactly why the **40–50 g protein after** matters.

## Resistance training (for a novice)

- **Progressive overload is the whole game.** Muscle adapts to a demand that keeps rising —
  more weight, more reps, better control, over time. In a deficit, progression is the signal
  that tells the body to *keep* the muscle. Without it, a deficit sheds fat *and* muscle.
- **Newbie gains are a gift with a shelf life.** Early strength jumps are largely neurological
  (learning the movement) plus real early hypertrophy. Enjoy them, don't extrapolate them —
  the plan has to still be adding weight in month three, not just week two.
- **Full-body, 3× per week is close to ideal for a beginner.** High frequency per muscle,
  lots of practice on the key patterns, easy to recover from. Don't push him toward a
  6-day bodybuilder "bro split" — wrong tool for his stage.
- **Compounds first.** Squat/hinge, horizontal + vertical push, horizontal + vertical pull.
  They train the most muscle for the time and drive the most progress. Isolation (curls,
  lateral raises, pushdowns) is seasoning, not the meal.
- **Effective rep range is wide (~5–30 reps)** as long as sets are taken close to failure.
  Practically, **8–15 reps** is the productive, joint-friendly zone — his **3×12** sits right in it.
- **Proximity to failure:** most working sets should end with **~1–3 reps in reserve (RIR)**.
  As a beginner nailing technique, he can leave a touch more in the tank — form quality beats
  grinding ugly reps.
- **Tempo & control** build more muscle and protect joints than heaving the weight. A controlled
  lower (2–3 s) with no bouncing is worth more than an extra plate.
- **Rest 1.5–3 min** between hard compound sets — enough to actually push the next set. Rushing
  rest to "keep the heart rate up" sacrifices the training stimulus; do cardio for cardio.

## Progression model

- **Double progression** is the clean method and it's exactly what's already in the rules:
  pick a weight, add **reps** each session until you hit the top of the range across **all** sets
  (**3×12 clean**), *then* add load (**+2.5 kg**) and start climbing reps again from wherever you land.
- **Recommend the jump the moment the data earns it** — three clean twelves means it's time.
  If the last set only hit 10, the weight stays. Hold the line.
- **Smaller jumps for smaller muscles / joint-sensitive lifts.** +2.5 kg on a row is nothing;
  on a lateral raise or a tibia-loading movement it's a big relative jump — progress those on
  reps longer before adding load.
- **Deloads** every ~6–10 weeks, or sooner when performance stalls across sessions, sleep tanks,
  or joints complain: a lighter week (drop volume/intensity ~40–50%) lets adaptation catch up.
  In a deficit, recovery is slower, so deloads matter *more*, not less.
- **A stall isn't failure** — it's information. First check sleep, protein, and whether the
  deficit is too aggressive before blaming the program.

## Recovery

- **Sleep 7–9 h** is the highest-leverage recovery lever, full stop. Under-slept = worse lifts,
  worse appetite control, worse fat loss. If one thing improves, make it this.
- **Rest days are when muscle is built**, not lost. A beginner on full-body 3×/week has recovery
  built in — resist the urge to train every day.
- **Soreness (DOMS) ≠ a good workout, and ≠ injury.** Normal muscle soreness is dull, symmetrical,
  eases with movement, gone in 24–72 h. **Sharp, joint-centred, or one-sided pain is a stop sign.**
- **Stress and life load** draw from the same recovery bank as training. High-stress weeks are
  when to hold weights steady rather than push PRs.

## Cardio & steps

- **Steps are the quiet workhorse of fat loss.** Walking burns calories without meaningfully
  adding to recovery debt — Jamie bookending sessions with indoor walks is exactly right.
  A daily step target (his is ~6,000+) is one of the best levers when the trend stalls.
- **Don't drown a deficit in cardio.** Hard, high-volume cardio on top of a calorie deficit eats
  into recovery and can cost muscle. Walking and some easy **Zone 2** (conversational pace) is
  plenty alongside lifting. Lifting protects the muscle; cardio and steps widen the deficit.

## Body recomposition — what to expect

- For a **novice in a deficit with enough protein and progressive lifting**, losing fat while
  gaining (or fully keeping) muscle is not just possible, it's the *expected* outcome. Jamie is
  the textbook case for it.
- **The scale will lie about this.** Recomp means fat down and muscle up, so the scale can stall
  or even tick up for a stretch while the body genuinely improves. This is why **the scale is one
  input, not the verdict.**
- **Track more than weight:** the 7-day weight trend, **waist measurement**, **body-fat %** off the
  smart scale (treat as a rough trend, not gospel), **strength progression** (the strength index),
  how clothes fit, and progress photos. When the scale sulks, these tell the real story.

## Joint-aware training (the tibia protocol)

- **Technique and control before load, always** — this is doubly true for Jamie's lower body.
  Starting the squat light on purpose was correct; it's not timidity, it's building the movement
  before loading it.
- **Progress joint-sensitive lifts on reps and range first, load last.** Earn the weight jump.
- **Pain rules:** working through *muscle* fatigue is training; working through *joint* pain is
  damage. Sharp, sudden, or lingering pain → stop the lift, don't push, and if it persists refer
  him to a professional. **Clive is a trainer, not a physio** — coach around it, don't diagnose it.
- Prefer **joint-friendly variations** (box squats, machine/cable work, controlled ranges) over
  ego-loaded free-weight maxing while he builds a base.

## Metrics that matter (and the ones that don't)

- **Matter:** 7-day weight trend, waist, body-fat trend, strength index / working weights,
  protein floor hit-rate, session consistency, sleep.
- **Noise:** any single day's scale reading, daily calorie precision to the gram, soreness level,
  "toning" language, supplement minutiae.

## Boundaries

- **None of this is medical advice.** Anything involving pain, injury, medication, or a health
  condition → tell Jamie to see a GP/physio. Coach around it; never diagnose or prescribe.
- **Don't over-claim.** Ranges and "roughly", not false precision. The body isn't a spreadsheet.
- When Jamie's data disagrees with a general principle here, **his data wins** — this doc guides
  the *default*; the numbers in `fitness-data.json` decide the *call*.
